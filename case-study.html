Observed pain hypothesis

A physiotherapist loses high-value clinical and business time after or between appointments creating session notes, home exercise programs, follow-up messages, and adherence checks. This creates workflow drag, inconsistent patient follow-through, and reduced capacity for care quality or revenue-generating sessions.

Human-in-the-loop boundary: the startup should not autonomously diagnose, prescribe, or replace clinical judgment. The initial wedge should support documentation, patient communication, adherence visibility, and administrative follow-through under physiotherapist control.

Initial customer and buyer map

  • User: physiotherapist doing notes, home exercise planning, patient follow-up, and progress review.
  • Buyer: solo clinic owner, clinic manager, practice principal, or department leader.
  • Beneficiary: patient who needs clearer instructions and better adherence support.
  • Trigger: overloaded appointment day, post-session admin backlog, missed patient follow-through, or poor visibility into home exercise adherence.
  • Alternatives: paper notes, EMR templates, generic exercise libraries, messaging apps, spreadsheets, manual reminders, and memory.

Layered hierarchy

Mission → outcomes → modules → components → tasks.

Use this decomposition to avoid treating the idea as one vague project. Every module has a goal, objective, concrete tasks, artifacts, and evidence standard.

Level 0

Founder growth mission

Become an evidence-driven founder by applying the framework to a real, high-trust workflow with real users, constraints, and commercial pressure.

Level 1

Startup project objective

Discover whether physiotherapists have an urgent, budgeted, repeated workflow pain around documentation, home exercise follow-up, and patient adherence.

Level 2

Evidence target

Move from anecdote to proof: repeated pain stories, current workarounds, budget owner, paid pilot willingness, repeated use, and measurable time saved.

M0. Personal founder OSGrowth

Goal: turn this project into a personal growth engine.

Tasks: run the founder diagnostic, create decision journal, set weekly review, define learning KPIs, and schedule customer-contact blocks.

Artifacts: diagnostic baseline, decision log, weekly review template, evidence scoreboard.

M1. Domain immersionDiscovery

Goal: understand the physiotherapist workflow before proposing a solution.

Tasks: observe sessions where possible, shadow admin work, collect workflow screenshots or descriptions, and define the exact moment the pain occurs.

Evidence: repeated stories of time pressure, frustration, workarounds, and consequences.

M2. Customer discoveryCustomer

Goal: validate pain, frequency, cost, current alternatives, and buying process.

Tasks: conduct 15–25 interviews with solo PTs, clinic owners, clinic staff, and patients; avoid pitching; score pain intensity and urgency.

Artifacts: interview notes, pain evidence table, stakeholder map, quote bank.

M3. JTBD and workflow mapProduct

Goal: identify the job customers are hiring current tools or habits to perform.

Tasks: map pre-session, in-session, post-session, home exercise, reminder, adherence, and reassessment flows.

Artifacts: JTBD job stories, workflow map, failure-point map, time-cost estimate.

M4. Market and stakeholder analysisMarket

Goal: determine whether the segment is reachable, budgeted, and structurally attractive.

Tasks: segment solo clinics, small clinics, multi-location clinics, hospitals, and sports rehab; map buyer, user, champion, procurement, and trust barriers.

Artifacts: market memo, segment scorecard, buyer-user-budget map.

M5. Alternatives and competitionCompetition

Goal: understand why existing tools are insufficient and where a wedge exists.

Tasks: teardown EMR templates, exercise prescription tools, patient apps, messaging workflows, generic note tools, and manual workarounds.

Artifacts: competitor matrix, substitute map, complaints log, switching-cost analysis.

M6. Wedge and value propositionStrategy

Goal: choose the smallest beachhead with urgent pain and a credible expansion path.

Tasks: score wedges such as post-session documentation, home exercise follow-up, adherence tracking, discharge summaries, and patient progress reports.

Artifacts: wedge scorecard, 10x value proposition, positioning statement.

M7. MVP and pilot designMVP

Goal: test behavior before building a full product.

Tasks: design concierge, clickable prototype, or limited AI-assisted workflow; define success and failure thresholds; recruit 3–5 pilot clinics.

Evidence: paid pilot, repeated weekly use, time saved, fewer missed follow-ups, and willingness to continue.

M8. AI-native workflow and evalsAI

Goal: use AI where it improves speed or quality without outsourcing clinical responsibility.

Tasks: prototype note summarization, exercise-plan drafting support, reminder copy, patient-check-in synthesis, and human review controls.

Artifacts: prompt library, 100-case eval set, failure-mode checklist, privacy/trust review.

M9. Founder-led sales and GTMSales

Goal: discover whether the pain converts into buying behavior.

Tasks: write outreach, run discovery calls, ask for pilot commitment, test pricing, track objections, and document sales cycle friction.

Artifacts: outreach tracker, call script, objection library, pricing memo, pilot agreement draft.

M10. Measurement and iterationLearning

Goal: turn every week into better judgment and better product direction.

Tasks: review interviews, pilot behavior, time saved, patient adherence signals, retention, and willingness to pay.

Artifacts: weekly evidence review, decision journal, pivot/persevere criteria, experiment backlog.

M11. Scale and company OSScale

Goal: scale only after repeatable value is visible.

Tasks: design onboarding, support, compliance workflow, team cadence, quality review, product roadmap, and repeatable channel tests.

Evidence: retention, repeatable onboarding, consistent outcomes, and known bottleneck.

Importance and urgency

Do the risk-reducing work before the product-building work.

The highest-priority work is not the most exciting work. It is the work that can falsify the project fastest or expose the right wedge.

P0 — Now existential evidence

  • Replace the placeholder pain thesis with the exact observed event, quote, and workflow moment.
  • Write the assumption stack: customer, pain, frequency, cost, budget, access, trust, product feasibility, and repeat usage.
  • Interview 5 physiotherapists without pitching and document past behavior only.
  • Map current alternatives and workarounds before inventing features.
  • Define the riskiest assumption and one test that can fail within 7 days.

P1 — This week segment and wedge

  • Run 10 more interviews across solo PTs, small clinics, and clinic owners.
  • Score segments on pain, urgency, budget, reachability, regulation/trust burden, and adoption friction.
  • Teardown 10 direct and indirect alternatives.
  • Select one wedge and write a 10x value proposition.
  • Draft a concierge pilot and outreach message.

P2 — Weeks 3–6 pilot learning

  • Recruit 3–5 pilot users or clinics.
  • Run manual or semi-automated workflow support before full automation.
  • Measure time saved, follow-up completion, repeated usage, and willingness to pay.
  • Create AI eval cases from real notes, edge cases, and failure modes after appropriate consent and de-identification.
  • Review pilot behavior weekly and decide whether to persevere, narrow, or pivot.

P3 — Later scale candidates

  • Build integrations only after the workflow is proven manually.
  • Automate only stable, repeated, well-understood steps.
  • Test repeatable acquisition channels after clear positioning.
  • Create onboarding, support, security, and quality-review routines.
  • Hire or partner only after the bottleneck is known.

12-week applied plan

Use the physiotherapist case as the practice vehicle.

Each week produces a concrete artifact and a decision. Reading and AI-generated analysis do not count unless they change what you do next.

Week 1

Problem thesis and founder OS

Exact observed-pain memo, assumption stack, diagnostic baseline, decision journal, interview guide.

Week 2

Customer discovery sprint

5–10 physiotherapist interviews, pain evidence table, current alternatives list, no-pitch self-critique.

Week 3

JTBD and workflow mapping

Workflow map, job stories, failure-point map, time-cost estimate, patient follow-through hypotheses.

Week 4

Market and stakeholder memo

Segment scorecard, buyer/user/champion map, channel hypotheses, trust and adoption risks.

Week 5

Alternatives teardown

10 direct/indirect alternative teardowns, complaint patterns, switching costs, wedge candidates.

Week 6

Wedge and MVP design

Wedge scorecard, 10x value proposition, riskiest assumption, concierge or prototype experiment card.

Week 7

Prototype and pilot setup

Manual workflow, clickable prototype or AI-assisted draft flow; success thresholds and pilot recruitment list.

Week 8

Founder-led sales

20 outreach messages, 10 discovery or sales calls, objection library, pilot commitment asks, pricing memo.

Week 9

Pilot execution

3–5 pilot users, weekly usage log, time-saved measurement, before/after workflow analysis.

Week 10

AI evals and trust controls

100-case eval set, failure-mode checklist, human-review policy, privacy and consent boundary memo.

Week 11

Validation decision

Evidence review, retention signal, willingness-to-pay analysis, pivot/persevere decision journal entry.

Week 12

Integration dossier

Full startup discovery dossier, roadmap, next experiments, advisor feedback request, next 12-week plan.

Evidence gates

Advance only when the evidence changes quality.

Gate 1

Problem evidence

At least 10 independent conversations show repeated pain, current workarounds, and a meaningful cost of inaction.

Gate 2

Wedge signal

One segment scores highest on pain, urgency, budget, reachability, and adoption feasibility.

Gate 3

MVP behavior

Users complete the workflow repeatedly without founder pressure and report a clear before/after improvement.

Gate 4

Commercial signal

A buyer commits to a paid pilot, budget discussion, renewal, or concrete next step with decision authority.

Gate 5

Trust signal

Users accept the human-review, privacy, security, and accountability model for the intended workflow.

Gate 6

Repeatability

The same value proposition, onboarding path, and support process work for multiple similar customers.

Gate 7

Founder learning

Your memos become more specific, behavioral, commercial, evidence-based, and decision-oriented.

Gate 8

Scale readiness

Retention, onboarding, and unit economics look plausible enough to test repeatable acquisition.
