Personal founder curriculum

Build founder judgment through disciplined reps.

A 12-week curriculum for a beginner with high ambition, limited industry experience, and a desire to build strong judgment rather than use AI as a shortcut.

Start the 12-week plan Apply to the physiotherapist case Read the research brief

Operating principle

You become a founder by repeatedly converting uncertainty into evidence, evidence into judgment, and judgment into action.

1

The 10 capabilities to build

1

Customer discovery

Understand real problems without pitching, leading, or projecting.

2

Market judgment

Evaluate urgency, budget, timing, access, regulation, and competition.

3

Problem selection

Choose problems worth obsession: frequent, painful, expensive, underserved, budgeted, and reachable.

4

Product judgment

Decide what should exist, what should not exist, and what creates real user value.

5

Sell customers, users, recruits, partners, investors, and belief.

6

Ship, test, follow up, measure, decide, and avoid preparation loops.

7

Understand enough to reason about APIs, data, security, LLMs, agents, evals, and reliability.

8

Strategic thinking

Choose where to play, how to win, what not to do, and what advantage can compound.

9

Use memos, specs, decision logs, and updates to sharpen thought.

10

Leadership and resilience

Lead through uncertainty with calm, accountability, ethics, and stamina.

2

The correct learning order

Start here

  1. Startup fundamentals: search vs execution, uncertainty, business models.
  2. Customers and problems: interviews, pain, alternatives, buyer/user distinction.
  3. Markets: timing, budget, competition, regulation, channels, switching costs.
  4. Products and MVPs: use cases, specs, activation, retention, smallest useful tests.

Then build leverage

  1. Sales and distribution: outreach, discovery, objection handling, pricing, follow-up.
  2. Business models and unit economics: CAC, LTV, margin, payback, churn.
  3. AI-native workflows: agents, coding assistants, RAG, evals, human review.
  4. Strategy and leadership: moats, constraints, recruiting, negotiation, culture.

4

Real-world exercises and AI use

Customer problem interviewsDiscovery

Run 5 interviews per week. Ask about the last time the problem happened, what they did, what it cost, who was involved, and what happens if they do nothing.

AI use: draft guides, summarize transcripts, cluster pain points.

Avoid: letting AI invent customer truth.

Market memoMarket

Write one memo per week covering segments, buyer/user, alternatives, budget, timing, regulation, competitors, channels, risks, and open questions.

AI use: structure the memo and identify missing stakeholders.

Avoid: accepting AI market claims without evidence.

Competitor teardownCompetition

Analyze target customer, promise, pricing, wedge, complaints, distribution, switching costs, and vulnerabilities.

AI use: summarize public positioning and compare alternatives.

Avoid: copying features without insight.

MVP design drillMVP

Define the hypothesis, riskiest assumption, smallest test, success threshold, failure threshold, time limit, and next decision.

AI use: generate MVP variants and identify fake validation risks.

Avoid: building a mini full product.

Founder-led sales repsSales

Write 20 targeted outreach messages, send at least 10, track responses, run calls, and document objections.

AI use: personalize outreach and simulate objections.

Avoid: generic AI spam.

AI workflow buildAI-native

Build one AI-assisted workflow per week with clear input, output, quality bar, human review point, and failure modes.

AI use: build, test, document, and evaluate the workflow.

Avoid: automating unclear workflows.

8

12-week curriculum

Week 1

Startup foundations

Startup definition memo, 5 wedge teardowns, founder notebook structure.

Week 2

Customer discovery

5 interview notes, pain point table, interview self-critique.

Week 3

JTBD and pain analysis

10 JTBD teardowns, 5 job stories, painkiller/vitamin ranking.

Week 4

Market research

Market memo, segment table, buyer/user/budget map.

Week 5

Competition and alternatives

10 competitor teardowns, alternative map, vulnerability memo.

Week 6

Wedges and MVPs

Wedge scorecard, 5 MVP experiment cards, riskiest assumption map.

Week 7

Product thinking

2 product specs, 3 product teardowns, feature prioritization matrix.

Week 8

Sales and outreach

Outreach scripts, response tracker, objection library, call self-review.

Week 9

Business models

5 business model teardowns, unit economics sheet, pricing memo.

Week 10

AI-native workflows

2 AI workflows, 30-case eval set, failure mode checklist.

Week 11

Strategy and leadership

2 strategy memos, decision journal entries, bottleneck analysis.

Week 12

Integration sprint

Full startup discovery dossier and next 12-week plan.

9

Weekly deliverables

Minimum5 conversations/attempts, 1 memo, 3 teardowns, 1 experiment design, 1 decision entry, 1 review.
Strong5–10 conversations, 10–20 outreach messages, 5 teardowns, 1 product spec, 1 prototype, 1 AI workflow improvement.
StandardA week only counts if it produces evidence, artifacts, and a decision—not just reading notes.

10–12

Templates and founder operating system

Decision journal

Date; decision; type; context; options; evidence; assumptions; risks; skeptic counterargument; decision; success criteria; review date; post-decision review.

Insight log

Date; source; observation; evidence; interpretation; confidence; missing evidence; implication; action; related assumption; follow-up question.

Weekly founder review

What did I do? What did I ship? Who did I talk to? What surprised me? What changed? What did I avoid? What is next week's experiment?

Founder OS databases

Markets, interviews, problems, competitors, experiments, product specs, sales/outreach, AI workflows, decisions, and insights.

Personal rules

No week without real people. No research without output. No AI conclusion without verification. No MVP without an assumption. No confusing motion with progress.

Improvement signals

Your work becomes more specific, behavioral, commercial, evidence-based, and decision-oriented.
